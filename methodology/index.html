🧠 Trading Scanner Methodology

This methodology explains how the Indian and USA scanners identify, score, rank, display, and track short-term trading ideas. The latest build now reflects the actual production scanner design: Swing and Intraday modes, NIFTY/SPY market-bias filters, earnings protection for USA stocks, Swing fallback logic, priority-separated tracking, and combination filters on the performance dashboard.

Updated for priority-separated performance tracking and Swing fallback logic

1. Core Objective

The scanners are designed to turn a broad watchlist into a smaller list of structured trading candidates. They do not predict certainty. They organize candidates by trend alignment, benchmark-relative strength or weakness, volume, volatility, entry proximity, stop placement, target path, and risk control.

The practical goal is to answer a few questions clearly: Is the broad market supportive? Is the stock aligned with the market direction? Is the setup near a trigger? Is the stop and target defined? Is the score high enough to treat the idea as a trade candidate or only as a watchlist idea?

2. Scanner Modes

Swing Mode

Uses daily candles. It is designed for multi-day ideas where entry, stop, and target are tracked after the idea is logged. Swing is allowed to use a fallback pass if the normal scan returns no candidates.

Intraday Mode

Uses 15-minute candles. It remains stricter and does not use the same loosened fallback logic, because it already tends to produce more frequent candidates.

Indian Scanner

Uses NIFTY as the main benchmark. If the index trend is bearish, fresh candidates must be SELL ideas. If bullish, fresh candidates must be BUY ideas.

USA Scanner

Uses SPY as the main benchmark. It also excludes individual stocks with earnings expected within the blackout window. ETFs such as SPY and QQQ are not blocked for earnings.

3. Market Bias Filter

The broad-market filter is the first discipline layer. The scanner should not fight the main index direction.

  • Bullish market bias: Fresh candidates must be BUY setups.
  • Bearish market bias: Fresh candidates must be SELL setups.
  • Mixed market bias: Candidate quality must be stronger before the idea is allowed through.
This rule is intentionally strict. It prevents cases where a bullish index environment produces bearish Top 3 trades, or a bearish index environment produces random bullish trades.

4. Normal Scan vs Swing Fallback

The scanner first runs a normal scan. If Swing mode returns zero fresh candidates, a second relaxed Swing fallback pass may run. This is designed to avoid a completely empty dashboard while still respecting market direction.

Normal Scan

Uses the standard trend, RSI, relative-strength, volume, ATR, trigger-distance, extension, and risk filters.

Swing Fallback

Runs only when Swing has no normal candidates. It relaxes select filters such as EMA-stack strictness, trigger distance, volume, and RSI window.

Direction Still Enforced

Fallback does not allow trades against NIFTY or SPY direction. Bearish benchmark still means SELL only; bullish benchmark still means BUY only.

Labelled Separately

Fallback candidates are labelled with Scan Type = Fallback so their future performance can be reviewed separately from normal candidates.

5. Setup Scoring Factors

Each candidate receives a setup-quality score based on multiple technical components. The score is not a probability of profit.

Trend Alignment

The model checks whether price is aligned with EMA20 and EMA50 and whether that structure supports the trade direction.

Relative Strength / Weakness

BUY candidates should outperform the benchmark. SELL candidates should underperform the benchmark.

RSI Window

The scanner prefers healthy momentum zones and avoids extreme overbought or oversold conditions where reversal risk may be higher.

Volume Ratio

Volume is compared with recent average volume. Higher participation improves setup quality; low participation weakens it.

ATR Volatility

ATR is used to avoid candidates that are too quiet to move or too volatile to size responsibly.

Trigger Distance

Ideas closer to their trigger are more actionable. Ideas too far from entry may be filtered out or downgraded.

Extension Control

The scanner avoids candidates that have already moved too far from their moving-average structure.

Risk Percentage

Structure-aware stops are checked so the trade is not impractically wide relative to the planned entry.

6. Priority Labels

Priority labels classify how strongly the scanner ranks a candidate. The label is used in both the scanner pages and the performance dashboard.

Scanner Area Highest Priority Medium Priority Low Priority Notes

India Intraday 90–94 84–89 78–83 Kept stricter because Intraday already produces more frequent ideas.

The conviction score is setup quality, not probability of success. A score of 94 does not mean a 94% chance of winning. Scores are capped because no trade is guaranteed or loss-proof.

7. Entry, Stop, Target, and Position Sizing

Each scanner candidate includes an entry trigger, stop-loss, target, risk/reward ratio, quantity, and trade value.

  • Entry: BUY entries trigger above price structure; SELL entries trigger below price structure.
  • Stop-loss: Uses ATR and recent swing structure so the stop is not placed randomly.
  • Target: Uses risk-based target logic, typically around 2R in the current build.
  • Position sizing: Quantity is limited by risk per trade and maximum allocation cap.
  • Confirmation: The dashboard notes that entry should be considered only after the trigger breaks with confirmation.
Wider structure-aware stops reduce position quantity. This is intentional. The scanner controls rupee/dollar risk rather than forcing the same number of shares on every setup.

8. USA Earnings Blackout

The USA scanner excludes individual stocks if earnings are expected within the next 8 calendar days. This reduces the risk of gap moves that can bypass clean technical structure.

  • Stocks with earnings inside the blackout window are skipped from fresh candidates.
  • If earnings date is unavailable, the scanner marks it but does not automatically block the stock.
  • ETFs are treated as earnings-not-applicable.

9. Performance Tracking

Each scanner logs its Top 3 ideas into a performance CSV. The scanner then refreshes the log on future runs to track whether the entry triggered, whether stop or target was hit first, current price, stop distance, target distance, result, R, and days.

  • Pending Trigger: Price has not yet crossed the entry level.
  • Active After Trigger: Entry triggered, but neither stop nor target has been hit yet.
  • Target Hit First: Target was reached before stop after entry.
  • Stop Hit First: Stop was reached before target after entry.
  • Ambiguous: Candle data does not reliably show whether stop or target happened first.

10. Priority-Separated Performance Dashboard

The master performance dashboard now separates Highest Priority ideas from Medium/Low Priority ideas. This matters because the true strategy should be judged on whether its strongest ranked ideas outperform the lower-ranked watchlist ideas.

Highest Priority Performance

Shows total ideas, closed win rate, average R, and closed win/loss count for the strongest candidate bucket.

Medium + Low Performance

Groups secondary candidates together so they do not distort the performance view of the core strategy.

Backfilled Priority

Older CSV rows without a Priority column are classified from conviction score so historical data remains usable.

Combination Filters

The dashboard supports combined filters such as USA + Swing, India + Intraday, Highest Priority + Losses, or USA + Swing + Highest Priority.

11. How to Read the Performance Dashboard

The combined win rate is useful, but it can hide the real signal. The better review process is to segment performance.

  • Review Highest Priority separately from Medium/Low.
  • Review India separately from USA.
  • Review Swing separately from Intraday.
  • Review BUY and SELL behavior separately by reading the combined log.
  • Review Average R, not just win rate.
  • Watch whether a 2R model remains positive after stop-first and target-first outcomes are tracked.
A 40% win rate can still be positive at 2R only if losses are controlled near -1R and winners reliably reach +2R. The dashboard exists to test that assumption, not to assume it is true.

12. Same-Candle Ambiguity

If available candle data shows that entry, stop, and target may have been touched within the same candle, the scanner does not assume a clean win or loss. It marks the outcome as ambiguous/review instead.

This is especially important for daily candles and 15-minute candles because OHLC data does not always reveal the exact sequence of price movement inside a candle.

13. Practical Use Rules

  • Do not treat the scanner as an automatic buy/sell engine.
  • Do not enter before the trigger level breaks with confirmation.
  • Do not interpret conviction score as probability of profit.
  • Respect benchmark direction. Avoid fighting NIFTY or SPY trend.
  • Review news, earnings, macro events, liquidity, and sector behavior before acting.
  • Use Priority-separated performance to decide whether the strategy is improving or deteriorating.
  • Use Medium/Low candidates primarily as watchlist ideas unless performance later proves they deserve trade status.

14. Current Known Limitations

No scanner can remove market risk. Technical setups can fail because of earnings, macro news, gaps, liquidity shocks, false breakouts, sudden sector rotation, or broad-market reversals. Public market data can be delayed, missing, or revised.

The scanner should be treated as a structured research assistant and performance-tracking system, not as a substitute for professional financial advice, discretionary judgment, or independent risk management.
